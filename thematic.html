Key Cross-Cutting Findings

  • Instructors strongly prioritize differentiated, level-wise activities — this is the #1 predictor of preference across both subjects
  • Option 1 (with principle + progression) won decisively on complex, multi-level queries (e.g., mixed-class design, activity sequencing)
  • Option 2 (manual only) won on later-stage, familiar-domain questions where instructors felt they didn't need principles explained
  • Concrete materials (tilli-bundle, TLM) are considered essential context for Maths — their absence makes responses feel incomplete
  • Language instructors care most about all children being able to participate, not just advanced learners
  • Several responses in both subjects were criticized for not advancing beyond what instructors already know ("this is what we already do")
  • Instructors use day-number as a proxy for child readiness — responses ignoring this context are seen as generic
  • The biggest gap: bot responses rarely suggest moving beyond the curriculum script when children have progressed

Instructors believe children speak and write more when given personally familiar or everyday topics. Abstract topics or curriculum-disconnected prompts are seen as barriers to expression, especially for early-level children.

Cluster F · Both Subjects

High-Quality Response Signals
What makes instructors say "this is exactly right"
Levels are named and given distinct tasks
Each child group (0-9, 10-99, addition, subtraction, Beginner, Letter, Word, Para, Story) receives a specific activity.
Day-appropriate materials are specified
TLM matches the day — concrete (tilli) for early days, abstract (word problems without support) for later days.
Teacher role is specified
Response says what the teacher does while groups work — who to observe, when to intervene, how to rotate.
Progression is embedded in the answer
The answer scaffolds: start simple, go complex. "First without borrow, then with borrow" is an example instructors praised.
